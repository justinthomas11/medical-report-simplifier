LIPID PANEL — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
A lipid panel (also called a lipid profile or cholesterol test) measures the levels of fats and fatty substances in your blood. It is a key test for assessing cardiovascular risk and is used to monitor heart health.

COMPONENTS AND NORMAL RANGES:

1. Total Cholesterol:
   - Desirable: Less than 200 mg/dL
   - Borderline High: 200-239 mg/dL
   - High: 240 mg/dL and above
   - Total cholesterol is the sum of all cholesterol types in your blood
   - Higher levels increase risk of heart disease and stroke

2. LDL Cholesterol (Low-Density Lipoprotein) — "Bad Cholesterol":
   - Optimal: Less than 100 mg/dL
   - Near Optimal: 100-129 mg/dL
   - Borderline High: 130-159 mg/dL
   - High: 160-189 mg/dL
   - Very High: 190 mg/dL and above
   - LDL carries cholesterol TO arteries, causing plaque buildup
   - Main target of cholesterol-lowering therapy
   - High LDL is the primary risk factor for atherosclerosis

3. HDL Cholesterol (High-Density Lipoprotein) — "Good Cholesterol":
   - Low (risk factor): Less than 40 mg/dL (men), Less than 50 mg/dL (women)
   - Desirable: 40-59 mg/dL
   - Optimal (protective): 60 mg/dL and above
   - HDL carries cholesterol AWAY from arteries to the liver for removal
   - Higher HDL is protective against heart disease
   - Exercise, healthy fats, and moderate alcohol can raise HDL

4. Triglycerides:
   - Normal: Less than 150 mg/dL
   - Borderline High: 150-199 mg/dL
   - High: 200-499 mg/dL
   - Very High: 500 mg/dL and above
   - Triglycerides store unused calories as fat
   - High levels associated with heart disease, diabetes, pancreatitis
   - Affected by diet, alcohol, obesity, and medications

5. VLDL Cholesterol (Very Low-Density Lipoprotein):
   - Normal: 2-30 mg/dL
   - Calculated as triglycerides divided by 5
   - Carries triglycerides through the bloodstream

6. Non-HDL Cholesterol:
   - Calculated as Total Cholesterol minus HDL
   - Desirable: Less than 130 mg/dL
   - Includes all "bad" types of cholesterol
   - Some doctors consider this a better predictor than LDL alone

7. Total Cholesterol/HDL Ratio:
   - Desirable: Less than 5:1
   - Optimal: 3.5:1
   - Higher ratio indicates greater cardiovascular risk

RISK FACTORS THAT AFFECT LIPID LEVELS:
- Diet high in saturated and trans fats
- Obesity and physical inactivity
- Smoking
- Diabetes
- Hypothyroidism
- Family history (familial hypercholesterolemia)
- Age (cholesterol tends to rise with age)
- Certain medications (steroids, beta-blockers, diuretics)

WHEN IS A LIPID PANEL ORDERED:
- Routine screening: every 4-6 years for healthy adults
- Annually for patients with cardiovascular risk factors
- Monitoring cholesterol-lowering medications (statins)
- Diabetes management
- After a heart attack or stroke

PREPARATION:
- Typically requires 9-12 hours of fasting before the test
- Only water is allowed during the fasting period
- Some newer guidelines allow non-fasting lipid panels

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- A single high reading doesn't mean you have heart disease
- Lifestyle changes (diet, exercise) are the first line of treatment
- Medications (statins) may be recommended based on overall risk assessment
- Results should be interpreted in the context of other risk factors
