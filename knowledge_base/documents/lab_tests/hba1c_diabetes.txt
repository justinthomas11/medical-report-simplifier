HbA1c AND DIABETES MONITORING — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c or A1c) measures the average blood sugar level over the past 2-3 months. It reflects the percentage of hemoglobin in red blood cells that has glucose attached to it. It is the primary test for diagnosing and monitoring diabetes.

HbA1c LEVELS AND INTERPRETATION:

1. Normal:
   - HbA1c: Below 5.7%
   - Estimated Average Glucose: Below 117 mg/dL
   - No diabetes

2. Prediabetes:
   - HbA1c: 5.7% to 6.4%
   - Estimated Average Glucose: 117-137 mg/dL
   - Increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes
   - Lifestyle changes can prevent or delay diabetes

3. Diabetes:
   - HbA1c: 6.5% or higher
   - Estimated Average Glucose: 140 mg/dL or higher
   - Diagnosis of diabetes (confirmed with repeat test)

4. Treatment Target (for most diabetics):
   - HbA1c: Below 7.0%
   - This target may vary based on individual circumstances
   - Elderly or those with complications may have a less strict target (7.5-8.0%)

HbA1c TO ESTIMATED AVERAGE GLUCOSE CONVERSION:
- 5.0% = ~97 mg/dL
- 5.5% = ~111 mg/dL
- 6.0% = ~126 mg/dL
- 6.5% = ~140 mg/dL
- 7.0% = ~154 mg/dL
- 7.5% = ~169 mg/dL
- 8.0% = ~183 mg/dL
- 9.0% = ~212 mg/dL
- 10.0% = ~240 mg/dL

RELATED BLOOD GLUCOSE TESTS:

1. Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG):
   - Normal: Less than 100 mg/dL
   - Prediabetes: 100-125 mg/dL
   - Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher
   - Measured after 8+ hours of fasting

2. Random Blood Glucose:
   - Normal: Usually below 140 mg/dL
   - Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher (with symptoms)
   - Can be taken at any time

3. Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT):
   - Normal: Less than 140 mg/dL at 2 hours
   - Prediabetes: 140-199 mg/dL at 2 hours
   - Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher at 2 hours
   - Measured 2 hours after drinking a glucose solution

4. Fasting Insulin:
   - Normal: 2-25 mIU/mL
   - High: Insulin resistance, early type 2 diabetes, PCOS
   - Low: Type 1 diabetes, advanced type 2 diabetes

COMPLICATIONS OF UNCONTROLLED DIABETES:
- Cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke)
- Nephropathy (kidney damage)
- Retinopathy (eye damage, blindness)
- Neuropathy (nerve damage, especially feet)
- Poor wound healing
- Increased infection risk

FACTORS THAT AFFECT HbA1c ACCURACY:
- Anemia or hemoglobin variants can give falsely high or low readings
- Recent blood transfusion
- Kidney disease
- Pregnancy
- Iron deficiency
- Certain hemoglobin disorders (sickle cell trait)

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- HbA1c should be tested every 3-6 months for diabetics
- A 1% decrease in HbA1c reduces complication risk by ~25%
- Diet, exercise, and medication all help lower HbA1c
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose provides day-to-day data
- HbA1c provides the big picture; daily testing provides the details
