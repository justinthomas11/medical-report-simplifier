ANTIHYPERTENSIVE MEDICATIONS — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Antihypertensives are medications used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension). Multiple drug classes are available, and many patients require combination therapy to reach blood pressure goals. Target is generally <130/80 mmHg for most patients.

FIRST-LINE MEDICATIONS:

1. ACE Inhibitors (-pril ending):
   - Examples: Lisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril, Benazepril
   - How it works: Blocks angiotensin-converting enzyme, relaxes blood vessels
   - Benefits: Heart and kidney protective; preferred in diabetes and heart failure
   - Side effects: Dry cough (10-20%), elevated potassium, rare angioedema
   - Contraindicated: Pregnancy, bilateral renal artery stenosis

2. ARBs (-sartan ending):
   - Examples: Losartan, Valsartan, Irbesartan, Telmisartan, Olmesartan
   - How it works: Blocks angiotensin II receptor, relaxes blood vessels
   - Benefits: Similar to ACE inhibitors but without the cough
   - Side effects: Elevated potassium, dizziness
   - Often used when ACE inhibitors cause cough

3. Calcium Channel Blockers:
   - Dihydropyridines: Amlodipine, Nifedipine — primarily lower blood pressure
   - Non-dihydropyridines: Diltiazem, Verapamil — also slow heart rate
   - How it works: Relaxes blood vessel walls by blocking calcium entry
   - Side effects: Ankle swelling (amlodipine), constipation (verapamil), dizziness
   - Good choice for elderly patients and those with angina

4. Thiazide Diuretics:
   - Examples: Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), Chlorthalidone, Indapamide
   - How it works: Increases urine output, reduces blood volume
   - Side effects: Low potassium, increased uric acid, increased blood sugar
   - Often used in combination with ACE inhibitors or ARBs
   - Chlorthalidone may be preferred over HCTZ (longer acting)

OTHER ANTIHYPERTENSIVES:

5. Beta-Blockers (-lol ending):
   - Examples: Metoprolol, Atenolol, Carvedilol, Bisoprolol, Propranolol
   - How it works: Slows heart rate and reduces heart's workload
   - Used for: Hypertension with heart failure, post-heart attack, atrial fibrillation
   - Side effects: Fatigue, slow heart rate, cold extremities, weight gain
   - Not first-line for hypertension alone (unless other indication exists)

6. Alpha-Blockers:
   - Examples: Doxazosin, Prazosin, Terazosin
   - How it works: Relaxes blood vessels by blocking alpha receptors
   - Also used for: Enlarged prostate (BPH)
   - Side effects: Dizziness, orthostatic hypotension (drop in BP when standing)

7. Aldosterone Antagonists:
   - Examples: Spironolactone, Eplerenone
   - How it works: Blocks aldosterone, reduces fluid retention
   - Used for: Resistant hypertension, heart failure
   - Side effects: Elevated potassium, gynecomastia (spironolactone)

BLOOD PRESSURE MONITORING:
- Home monitoring is recommended for all hypertensive patients
- Take readings at the same time daily
- Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measuring
- Take 2-3 readings 1 minute apart and average them
- Keep a log to share with your doctor

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- Never stop blood pressure medications suddenly
- Most medications take 2-4 weeks to show full effect
- Lifestyle changes complement medication therapy
- Many patients need 2-3 medications to reach target
- Regular follow-up is essential to adjust medications
