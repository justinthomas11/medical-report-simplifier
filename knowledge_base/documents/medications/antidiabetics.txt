COMMON ANTIDIABETIC MEDICATIONS — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Antidiabetic medications are used to manage blood sugar levels in people with diabetes. Type 1 diabetes always requires insulin. Type 2 diabetes management starts with lifestyle changes and metformin, with additional medications added as needed.

ORAL MEDICATIONS:

1. Metformin (Glucophage):
   - Class: Biguanide
   - First-line treatment for Type 2 diabetes
   - How it works: Decreases liver glucose production, improves insulin sensitivity
   - Typical dose: 500-2000 mg/day
   - Side effects: GI upset (nausea, diarrhea) — usually improves over time
   - Note: Should be stopped before contrast dye procedures; avoid in severe kidney disease

2. Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride:
   - Class: Sulfonylureas
   - How it works: Stimulates pancreas to produce more insulin
   - Side effects: Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), weight gain
   - Caution: Risk of low blood sugar, especially in elderly

3. Sitagliptin (Januvia), Linagliptin (Tradjenta):
   - Class: DPP-4 Inhibitors
   - How it works: Increases insulin release after meals, decreases glucagon
   - Side effects: Generally well tolerated; joint pain, pancreatitis (rare)

4. Empagliflozin (Jardiance), Dapagliflozin (Farxiga):
   - Class: SGLT2 Inhibitors
   - How it works: Causes kidneys to excrete excess glucose in urine
   - Benefits: Weight loss, blood pressure reduction, heart and kidney protection
   - Side effects: UTIs, yeast infections, dehydration
   - Important: Shown to reduce cardiovascular death and slow kidney disease progression

5. Pioglitazone (Actos):
   - Class: Thiazolidinediones (TZDs)
   - How it works: Improves insulin sensitivity in fat and muscle cells
   - Side effects: Weight gain, fluid retention, fracture risk
   - Caution: Avoid in heart failure

INJECTABLE MEDICATIONS:

6. Liraglutide (Victoza), Semaglutide (Ozempic), Dulaglutide (Trulicity):
   - Class: GLP-1 Receptor Agonists
   - How it works: Mimics GLP-1 hormone — increases insulin, decreases glucagon, slows digestion
   - Benefits: Weight loss, cardiovascular protection
   - Side effects: Nausea (usually temporary), pancreatitis (rare)
   - Given as weekly or daily injection

7. Insulin:
   - Required for all Type 1 diabetes; some Type 2 patients
   - Rapid-acting: Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog) — works in 15 min
   - Short-acting: Regular insulin — works in 30 min
   - Intermediate: NPH — lasts 12-18 hours
   - Long-acting: Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) — lasts 24 hours
   - Side effects: Hypoglycemia, weight gain, injection site reactions

KEY MONITORING:
- HbA1c every 3-6 months (target usually <7%)
- Fasting and post-meal blood glucose
- Kidney function (some medications require dose adjustment)
- Signs of hypoglycemia: shakiness, sweating, confusion, dizziness
