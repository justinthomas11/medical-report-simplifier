HYPERTENSION (HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE) — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Hypertension is a condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high. It is often called the "silent killer" because it usually has no symptoms but can lead to serious health complications if untreated. Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded as two numbers: systolic (pressure when heart beats) over diastolic (pressure between beats).

BLOOD PRESSURE CATEGORIES:

1. Normal:
   - Systolic: Less than 120 mmHg AND Diastolic: Less than 80 mmHg
   - Reading: <120/80 mmHg
   - No treatment needed, maintain healthy lifestyle

2. Elevated:
   - Systolic: 120-129 mmHg AND Diastolic: Less than 80 mmHg
   - Increased risk of developing hypertension
   - Lifestyle changes recommended

3. Stage 1 Hypertension:
   - Systolic: 130-139 mmHg OR Diastolic: 80-89 mmHg
   - Lifestyle changes plus medication may be considered
   - Based on cardiovascular risk assessment

4. Stage 2 Hypertension:
   - Systolic: 140 mmHg or higher OR Diastolic: 90 mmHg or higher
   - Requires lifestyle changes AND medication
   - Usually combination of two or more medications

5. Hypertensive Crisis:
   - Systolic: Higher than 180 mmHg AND/OR Diastolic: Higher than 120 mmHg
   - Medical emergency requiring immediate treatment
   - Can cause organ damage (brain, heart, kidneys, eyes)

RISK FACTORS:
- Age (risk increases with age)
- Family history
- Obesity
- Physical inactivity
- High sodium diet
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Smoking
- Stress
- Chronic kidney disease
- Diabetes
- Sleep apnea
- Race (more common and severe in African Americans)

SYMPTOMS (usually none — "silent killer"):
- Most people have no symptoms even with dangerously high levels
- Severe hypertension may cause: headaches, shortness of breath, nosebleeds, dizziness
- Hypertensive crisis: severe headache, chest pain, vision changes, difficulty breathing

COMPLICATIONS OF UNTREATED HYPERTENSION:
- Heart attack and heart disease
- Stroke
- Heart failure
- Kidney disease and failure
- Vision loss
- Peripheral artery disease
- Aneurysm
- Vascular dementia

MANAGEMENT:
- Lifestyle changes: DASH diet, reduce sodium (<2300mg/day), exercise, weight loss, limit alcohol, quit smoking
- First-line medications: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, thiazide diuretics
- Regular monitoring: Home blood pressure monitoring recommended
- Target: Generally <130/80 mmHg for most patients

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- Blood pressure should be measured on multiple occasions before diagnosis
- White coat hypertension (elevated only at doctor's office) is common
- Home blood pressure monitoring gives more accurate picture
- Lifestyle changes can reduce systolic BP by 5-15 mmHg
- Medication adherence is crucial — do not stop without consulting doctor
