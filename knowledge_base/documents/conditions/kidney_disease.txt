CHRONIC KIDNEY DISEASE (CKD) — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Chronic kidney disease is a long-term condition where the kidneys gradually lose their ability to filter waste and excess fluids from the blood. It affects approximately 15% of adults and is often associated with diabetes and hypertension. Early detection and management can slow progression.

STAGES OF CKD (based on eGFR):
- Stage 1 (eGFR 90+): Kidney damage with normal or high GFR. Usually no symptoms. Treat underlying cause.
- Stage 2 (eGFR 60-89): Mild decrease. Usually no symptoms. Monitor and manage risk factors.
- Stage 3a (eGFR 45-59): Mild-moderate decrease. May have mild symptoms. Active management needed.
- Stage 3b (eGFR 30-44): Moderate-severe decrease. Complications begin. Specialist referral recommended.
- Stage 4 (eGFR 15-29): Severe decrease. Prepare for renal replacement therapy. Nephrology management essential.
- Stage 5 (eGFR <15): Kidney failure. Dialysis or transplant needed.

KEY LAB VALUES TO MONITOR:
- eGFR: Primary measure of kidney function
- Creatinine: Rises as kidney function declines
- BUN: Rises with decreased kidney function
- Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR): Detects early kidney damage
- Potassium: May rise in advanced CKD
- Phosphorus: May rise in advanced CKD
- Calcium: May fall in advanced CKD
- Hemoglobin: Anemia common in CKD
- Bicarbonate: Metabolic acidosis in advanced CKD
- Parathyroid hormone (PTH): Rises in advanced CKD

RISK FACTORS:
- Diabetes (leading cause — 40% of CKD cases)
- Hypertension (second leading cause — 25% of CKD cases)
- Heart disease
- Family history of kidney disease
- Age over 60
- Obesity
- Smoking
- Ethnicity (higher risk in African Americans, Hispanics, Native Americans)

SYMPTOMS (often absent in early stages):
- Fatigue
- Swelling in feet and ankles (edema)
- Decreased urine output
- Shortness of breath
- Nausea and vomiting
- Loss of appetite
- Muscle cramps
- Itchy skin
- Sleep problems
- Confusion (in advanced stages)

COMPLICATIONS:
- Cardiovascular disease (leading cause of death in CKD)
- Anemia (kidneys produce less erythropoietin)
- Bone disease (mineral and bone disorder)
- Hyperkalemia (dangerous high potassium)
- Metabolic acidosis
- Fluid overload
- Uremia (toxic waste buildup)

MANAGEMENT:
- Blood pressure control: Target <130/80 mmHg. ACE inhibitors or ARBs preferred.
- Blood sugar control: HbA1c <7% if diabetic
- Diet: Low sodium, controlled protein, limit potassium and phosphorus in advanced stages
- Medications: Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, certain antibiotics)
- Newer therapies: SGLT2 inhibitors (shown to slow CKD progression)
- Dialysis: Hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis for Stage 5
- Transplant: Best long-term option for eligible patients

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- CKD is often called a "silent disease" — symptoms appear late
- Regular screening is crucial for high-risk individuals
- eGFR decline of >5 mL/min/year is considered rapid progression
- Early referral to nephrology improves outcomes
- CKD significantly increases cardiovascular risk
