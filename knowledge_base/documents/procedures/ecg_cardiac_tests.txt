ECG AND CARDIAC TESTS — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Cardiac tests evaluate the heart's structure, electrical activity, and function. The ECG (electrocardiogram) is the most common and fundamental cardiac test.

ECG / EKG (ELECTROCARDIOGRAM):

What it is:
- Records the electrical activity of the heart
- Quick (10 seconds), painless, non-invasive
- Uses electrodes placed on chest, arms, and legs
- Produces a tracing showing heart rhythm and electrical conduction

What ECG measures:
- Heart rate: Normal 60-100 bpm
- Heart rhythm: Regular or irregular
- Electrical conduction: How electrical signals travel through the heart
- Heart muscle: Signs of damage, enlargement, or strain

Normal ECG findings:
- Normal sinus rhythm (NSR)
- Regular rate and rhythm
- Normal P waves, QRS complexes, and T waves
- No ST segment changes
- Normal intervals (PR, QRS, QT)

Common abnormal findings:
- Sinus tachycardia: Heart rate >100 bpm (exercise, fever, anxiety, dehydration)
- Sinus bradycardia: Heart rate <60 bpm (athletes, medications, heart block)
- Atrial fibrillation (AFib): Irregular rhythm, no distinct P waves — increases stroke risk
- ST elevation: May indicate acute heart attack (STEMI) — emergency
- ST depression: May indicate ischemia (reduced blood flow to heart)
- T wave inversion: Can indicate ischemia, strain, or prior heart attack
- Bundle branch block (BBB): Delayed conduction — left or right
- Prolonged QT interval: Risk of dangerous arrhythmias
- Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH): Thickened heart muscle — often from hypertension

ECHOCARDIOGRAM (Echo):

What it is:
- Ultrasound of the heart
- Shows heart structure, valves, and how well heart pumps
- Non-invasive, no radiation

Key measurements:
- Ejection Fraction (EF): Normal 55-70%. Measures how well the left ventricle pumps
  - Mildly reduced: 40-54%
  - Moderately reduced: 30-40%
  - Severely reduced: <30%
- Valve function: Stenosis (narrowing) or regurgitation (leaking)
- Wall motion: Normal or abnormal movement
- Chamber sizes: Normal or dilated/enlarged
- Pericardial effusion: Fluid around the heart

STRESS TEST:

What it is:
- Evaluates heart function during physical exertion
- Exercise (treadmill/bicycle) or pharmacological (medication-induced) stress
- Can be combined with ECG, echo, or nuclear imaging

Types:
- Exercise stress test: Walk on treadmill with increasing speed/incline
- Pharmacological stress test: Drug (dobutamine/adenosine) simulates exercise — for those who can't exercise
- Nuclear stress test: Uses radioactive tracer to image blood flow to heart
- Stress echocardiogram: Echo before and after stress

Results:
- Normal/Negative: Heart responds normally to stress
- Abnormal/Positive: Suggests reduced blood flow to part of the heart (possible coronary artery disease)

CARDIAC BIOMARKERS (Blood Tests):

1. Troponin (I or T):
   - Most sensitive marker for heart muscle damage
   - Normal: <0.04 ng/mL (varies by assay)
   - Elevated: Heart attack (myocardial infarction), myocarditis, heart failure
   - Rises within 3-6 hours of heart attack, peaks at 24 hours

2. BNP / NT-proBNP:
   - Marker for heart failure
   - BNP normal: <100 pg/mL
   - NT-proBNP normal: <300 pg/mL
   - Elevated: Heart failure, kidney disease, pulmonary embolism

3. CK-MB:
   - Older marker for heart damage (largely replaced by troponin)
   - Can indicate heart attack or muscle damage

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- ECG is a snapshot — it shows the heart's electrical activity at that moment
- A normal ECG does not rule out heart disease
- Serial ECGs (repeated over time) can show changes
- Always correlate ECG findings with symptoms and other tests
- Chest pain with ECG changes is a medical emergency
