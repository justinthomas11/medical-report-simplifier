BLOOD WORK PROCEDURES AND GENERAL LAB INFORMATION — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Blood tests (blood work) are among the most common medical tests. They provide information about organ function, detect diseases, assess treatment effectiveness, and monitor health conditions.

COMMON BLOOD TEST PANELS:

1. Complete Blood Count (CBC):
   - Measures red blood cells, white blood cells, hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelets
   - Detects: Anemia, infection, blood disorders
   - Fasting: Not required

2. Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP):
   - Measures: Glucose, calcium, sodium, potassium, chloride, bicarbonate, BUN, creatinine
   - Assesses: Kidney function, blood sugar, electrolyte balance
   - Fasting: May be required (for glucose)

3. Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP):
   - Everything in BMP plus: ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin, albumin, total protein
   - Assesses: Kidney + liver function
   - Fasting: May be required

4. Lipid Panel:
   - Measures: Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides
   - Assesses: Cardiovascular risk
   - Fasting: 9-12 hours recommended

5. Thyroid Panel:
   - Measures: TSH, Free T4, sometimes Free T3
   - Assesses: Thyroid function
   - Fasting: Not required

6. HbA1c:
   - Measures: Average blood sugar over 2-3 months
   - Assesses: Diabetes control
   - Fasting: Not required

7. Iron Studies:
   - Measures: Serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, transferrin saturation
   - Assesses: Iron deficiency or overload
   - Fasting: Morning sample preferred

8. Coagulation Panel:
   - Measures: PT, INR, aPTT
   - Assesses: Blood clotting ability
   - Used for: Monitoring anticoagulant therapy, pre-surgery

9. Inflammatory Markers:
   - ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate): Normal <20 mm/hr
   - CRP (C-Reactive Protein): Normal <3 mg/L
   - Used to detect and monitor inflammation

PREPARATION FOR BLOOD TESTS:
- Fasting tests: No food or drink (except water) for 8-12 hours before
- Stay hydrated: Drinking water makes veins easier to find
- Inform your doctor about all medications and supplements
- Morning samples are preferred for many tests (hormones follow daily patterns)
- Avoid strenuous exercise before certain tests (can affect muscle enzymes)

UNDERSTANDING YOUR RESULTS:
- Reference range: The range of values found in 95% of healthy people
- Flagged values: "H" = high, "L" = low compared to reference range
- Slightly abnormal: May not be clinically significant
- Trends: Changes over time are often more important than single values
- Context: Results must be interpreted with symptoms and medical history

FACTORS THAT AFFECT RESULTS:
- Time of day (cortisol, testosterone are highest in morning)
- Recent meals (glucose, triglycerides affected by food)
- Medications (many drugs affect lab values)
- Exercise (can elevate CK, AST, LDH)
- Dehydration (concentrates blood, making values appear higher)
- Stress (can elevate glucose, cortisol, WBC)
- Pregnancy (changes many normal ranges)
- Age and sex (different normal ranges)
- Altitude (affects hemoglobin and hematocrit)

WHEN RESULTS ARE CONCERNING:
- Critical values: Lab will immediately notify your doctor
- Significantly abnormal: May require repeat testing to confirm
- Progressive changes: Worsening trends need investigation
- Multiple abnormalities: May point to a specific diagnosis

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- One abnormal test doesn't necessarily mean disease
- Repeat testing may be needed to confirm abnormal results
- Always discuss results with your healthcare provider
- Keep a record of your lab results to track trends
- Don't compare your results to others — normal ranges vary by individual factors
