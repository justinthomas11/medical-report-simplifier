MEDICAL IMAGING — X-RAY, CT SCAN, AND MRI — Reference Guide

OVERVIEW:
Medical imaging allows doctors to see inside the body without surgery. Different imaging methods use different technologies and are suited for different purposes.

X-RAY (RADIOGRAPH):

What it is:
- Uses small amounts of radiation to create images of internal structures
- Quick, painless, and widely available
- Best for: bones, chest, detecting fractures and lung conditions

Common X-ray types:
- Chest X-ray: Evaluates heart size, lungs, pneumonia, fluid, fractures
- Bone X-ray: Fractures, arthritis, bone infections, tumors
- Abdominal X-ray: Bowel obstruction, kidney stones, foreign bodies
- Dental X-ray: Tooth decay, infections, jaw conditions

Normal chest X-ray findings:
- Clear lung fields (no opacities or infiltrates)
- Normal heart size (cardiothoracic ratio <0.5)
- Normal mediastinum and trachea
- No pleural effusion
- Intact bony structures

Abnormal findings:
- Opacity/infiltrate: Pneumonia, tumor, fluid
- Cardiomegaly: Enlarged heart (heart failure)
- Pleural effusion: Fluid around the lungs
- Pneumothorax: Collapsed lung
- Fracture: Break in bone continuity

CT SCAN (COMPUTED TOMOGRAPHY):

What it is:
- Uses X-rays and computers to create detailed cross-sectional images
- More detailed than regular X-ray
- Can be done with or without contrast dye
- Scan time: Usually 5-30 minutes

Best for:
- Head/brain: Stroke, bleeding, tumors, trauma
- Chest: Pulmonary embolism, lung nodules, aortic conditions
- Abdomen: Appendicitis, kidney stones, liver/pancreas conditions, tumors
- Trauma: Quick assessment of injuries

Contrast dye:
- IV contrast: Enhances blood vessels and organs
- Oral contrast: Enhances GI tract
- Allergic reactions possible (inform doctor of prior reactions)
- Kidney function should be checked before IV contrast

Common CT findings:
- Normal: No acute abnormality, no mass, no hemorrhage
- Abnormal: Mass/lesion, hemorrhage, fracture, effusion, lymphadenopathy

MRI (MAGNETIC RESONANCE IMAGING):

What it is:
- Uses strong magnets and radio waves (NO radiation)
- Creates very detailed images of soft tissues
- Scan time: 30-90 minutes (patient must lie still)

Best for:
- Brain and spinal cord: Tumors, MS, stroke, disc herniation
- Joints: Ligament tears, cartilage damage, meniscus injuries
- Soft tissues: Muscles, tendons, organs
- Cardiac MRI: Heart structure and function

MRI safety:
- Cannot be done with certain metal implants (pacemakers, some clips)
- No radiation exposure
- Claustrophobia may be an issue (open MRI available)
- Loud during scanning (earplugs provided)
- Contrast (gadolinium) may be used — different from CT contrast

Advantages over CT:
- No radiation
- Better soft tissue detail
- Better for brain, spine, and joints

Disadvantages:
- Longer scan time
- More expensive
- Cannot be done with certain implants
- Less available in emergencies

ULTRASOUND:
- Uses sound waves (no radiation)
- Real-time imaging
- Best for: pregnancy, gallbladder, thyroid, heart (echocardiogram), kidneys, blood vessels
- No contrast needed (usually)
- Operator-dependent

IMPORTANT NOTES:
- Inform your doctor if you are pregnant before any imaging
- CT and X-ray involve radiation — benefit must outweigh risk
- MRI is safe during pregnancy (no radiation) but gadolinium contrast is avoided
- Always inform about allergies, kidney problems, and metal implants
- Results are interpreted by radiologists and reported to your doctor
